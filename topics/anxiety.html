2 What Is Anxiety?

Anxiety is an evolutionary survival mechanism our brain developed to keep us safe in the face of danger. It's not a sign of weakness or something that is inherently "wrong" with you. While this response was originally developed to help our ancestors survive physical threats, today it is often triggered by psychological challenges such as work, relationships, finances, health concerns, athletics, or social situations.

At its core, anxiety tends to emerge when three beliefs come together:

  1. We believe the outcome is important. The more something matters to us, the greater the emotional investment we have in the result. This could be a job interview, a championship game, a difficult conversation, an exam, or even how someone responds to a text message. When we attach significant meaning to an outcome, our brain naturally pays closer attention because it perceives that something valuable is at stake. In short, the greater the perceived threat, the more likely we are to experience anxiety.
  2. We believe the outcome is unpredictable. The human brain prefers certainty. We strive for safety, comfort, and familiarity. When we cannot accurately predict what will happen, our mind begins generating possibilities — many of which are negative. This uncertainty keeps the brain searching for answers and preparing for multiple scenarios. The less predictable we perceive a situation to be, the more vigilant and anxious we become.
  3. We believe the outcome is outside of our control. Perhaps the most powerful contributor to anxiety is the belief that we cannot influence what happens next. While we may be able to control our preparation, effort, attitude, or response, we cannot control other people's decisions, unexpected events, or final outcomes. When our attention becomes fixated on factors outside our control, the brain interprets the situation as a threat that we are unable to manage, causing anxiety to increase.

These three beliefs work together. If something is important but predictable and within your control, anxiety is usually manageable. If something is unpredictable but unimportant (think about a test worth 2% of your grade), it rarely creates significant distress. However, when an outcome feels highly important, highly uncertain, and outside your control, anxiety often reaches its highest levels.

Fortunately, this model also provides a roadmap for reducing anxiety. We can ask ourselves three questions:

  • Am I placing more importance on this outcome than it deserves?
  • Can I reduce uncertainty by preparing, gathering information, or making a plan?
  • What aspects of this situation are actually within my control, and how can I focus my energy there?

By shifting our attention toward controllable actions instead of uncontrollable outcomes, anxiety often begins to lose its intensity. Perspective reduces excessive importance. Preparation increases predictability. Focusing on our actions and responses restores a sense of control.

The goal is not to eliminate anxiety entirely. Some anxiety is both normal and useful — it can sharpen focus, increase motivation, and prepare the body for action. The goal is to understand why anxiety appears and to respond in a way that keeps it from controlling our thoughts, emotions, and behavior. When we recognize that anxiety is largely driven by our perceptions of importance, unpredictability, and control, we can begin changing those perceptions and, in turn, change our emotional experience.

4 Coping Skills for Anxiety

Once we recognize that anxiety often grows when we perceive something as important, unpredictable, and outside of our control, we can begin using strategies that reduce its intensity. Coping skills are not designed to eliminate anxiety completely (remember, we need some level of anxiety to keep us safe and prevent us from engaging in overtly dangerous behavior). Instead, they help us regulate our emotional and physiological response so that anxiety becomes manageable rather than overwhelming.

Like any skill, these techniques become more effective with practice. Many people mistakenly believe that coping skills "don't work" because they expect immediate relief or only try them during moments of intense anxiety. In reality, coping skills are most effective when they are practiced consistently, allowing them to become automatic when stress arises.

Below are seven evidence-based coping skills that can help reduce anxiety, why they work, and common barriers that may make them difficult to use.

Rhythmic Breathing

One of the first things anxiety affects is our breathing. During periods of stress, breathing often becomes rapid, shallow, and centered in the chest. This reinforces the body's fight-or-flight response and signals to the brain that danger is present.

Slow, controlled breathing — particularly breathing that emphasizes long, slow exhales — activates the parasympathetic nervous system, often referred to as the body's "rest and digest" system. This sends a message to the brain that the threat has decreased, allowing heart rate, muscle tension, and overall physiological arousal to gradually decrease.

While deep breathing can be highly effective, many people stop using it because they expect anxiety to disappear immediately. Like strengthening a muscle, breathing techniques become more effective with regular practice rather than only during moments of crisis.

In addition to deep, rhythmic breaths to slow down heart rate and regulate the nervous system, another helpful strategy is "Box Breaths." You can practice them here.

Anchoring/Grounding Techniques

Anxiety often pulls attention into the future through "what if" thinking, or due to a general sense of being overwhelmed. Grounding techniques help interrupt this cycle by redirecting attention back to the present moment.

One of the most effective grounding techniques is utilizing cold water. Taking a cold shower, or even splashing cold water on your face, can reduce physical symptoms of anxiety quickly. It's also difficult to have racing thoughts when you're freezing, as your nervous system slows down and brings your attention to find and focus on your breath. Other grounding techniques can involve noticing five things you see, four things you feel, three things you hear, two things you smell, and one thing you taste. Other methods include focusing on physical sensations, holding a cold object, or intentionally describing your surroundings in detail.

These techniques do not solve the problem causing anxiety, but they reduce the brain's tendency to become trapped in catastrophic thinking. By anchoring attention in the present, the nervous system has an opportunity to settle before returning to problem-solving.

One common barrier is that grounding can initially feel overly simple or ineffective. People sometimes dismiss it because it doesn't directly eliminate the stressful situation. However, its purpose is not to remove the problem — it is to reduce emotional intensity enough to think more clearly.

Challenge Unhelpful Thoughts

Our thoughts strongly influence how anxious we feel. When anxiety increases, the brain often overestimates danger while underestimating our ability to cope. This can lead to catastrophizing, assuming the worst, or treating unlikely outcomes as though they are inevitable.

Cognitive restructuring involves identifying these automatic thoughts, examining the evidence for and against them, and developing more balanced alternatives. Rather than replacing negative thoughts with unrealistic optimism, the goal is to think more accurately. I'm not suggesting that you make up a version of reality to feel better, but rather to stop making up thoughts that aren't based in truth in the first place.

For example, instead of thinking, "If I make one mistake, everything will fall apart," a more balanced thought might be, "Mistakes happen, and I've handled difficult situations successfully before."

The biggest challenge with this skill is that anxious thoughts often feel true simply because they are emotionally intense. Separating feelings from facts requires patience and repeated practice.

Focus on What You Can Control

A common saying in Psychology is: "The enemy of anxiety is action." A lot of anxious thoughts and symptoms occur due to the anticipation of a negative or uncertain outcome. Taking action allows us to focus on our response and occupies our minds in a way that doesn't allow time or space for anxiety to prevail.

One of the most effective ways to reduce anxiety is to intentionally shift attention toward factors within our control. This may include our preparation, effort, communication, attitude, daily habits, or how we respond regardless of the outcome. Anxiety naturally directs attention toward outcomes we cannot fully control — other people's opinions, future events, unexpected circumstances, or things that have not yet happened. When we're worried about outcomes that aren't in our control, taking action is difficult, and the responses we decide to move forward with are often misguided and counterproductive.

Identifying controllables then creating and following through with a plan of action increases confidence because it places our energy where it can actually make a difference. Although we cannot control every outcome, we almost always have control over our actions.

Many people struggle with this skill because worrying can create the illusion of productivity. Letting go of uncontrollable outcomes may initially feel irresponsible or as though we are "giving up," when in reality we are simply directing energy toward what is most effective.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Anxiety affects both the mind and the body. Many people experiencing anxiety unknowingly tighten their shoulders, jaw, hands, neck, or back throughout the day.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR) involves intentionally tensing and then relaxing different muscle groups throughout the body. This increases awareness of physical tension while encouraging the body to release it.

Reducing muscle tension often decreases physiological arousal, which in turn reduces emotional anxiety. The brain and body constantly communicate with one another. When the body begins to relax, the brain often follows.

Here is a guided PMR video that you can do on your own. Make sure you're in a comfortable, quiet, safe space to do so.

The main barrier is consistency. PMR can take several minutes to complete, and many individuals stop practicing because they feel too busy or don't notice dramatic results after only one or two attempts.

Physical Exercise

Regular physical activity is one of the most well-supported strategies for managing anxiety. Exercise helps regulate stress hormones, improves mood through the release of neurotransmitters such as endorphins, improves sleep, and builds confidence in one's ability to tolerate physical discomfort.

Cortisol is your body's alarm system hormone, often released during stressful situations to give you the needed burst of energy to execute a fight or flight response. The more stress and anxiety you experience, the higher cortisol levels your body takes on. High levels of cortisol over a long period of time can have severe mental and physical health ramifications including heart problems, weakened immune system, digestive issues, and depression. High cardiovascular workouts (getting your heart rate up) reduces resting cortisol levels overtime.

Exercise also provides a healthy outlet for the physical energy that often accompanies anxiety. Many symptoms of anxiety — including increased heart rate, muscle tension, and restlessness — are similar to the physical sensations experienced during exercise. Repeated exposure to these sensations in a safe environment can reduce fear of them over time.

Despite these benefits, anxiety itself often becomes the barrier. Low motivation, fatigue, perfectionism, or believing that exercise must be intense to be beneficial can prevent people from starting. In reality, even moderate activities such as walking can have meaningful effects on anxiety.

Mindfulness and Acceptance

Many people respond to anxiety by fighting it, avoiding it, or attempting to make it disappear immediately. Ironically, the harder we struggle against anxiety, the more attention and power it often receives.

Mindfulness teaches us to observe anxious thoughts, emotions, and physical sensations without immediately reacting to them. Rather than trying to eliminate anxiety, we learn to acknowledge its presence while continuing to engage in behaviors that align with our goals and values.

Acceptance does not mean liking anxiety or giving up. Instead, it means recognizing that uncomfortable emotions are a normal part of life and that we do not have to wait for anxiety to disappear before taking meaningful action. A combination of challenging harmful thoughts while practicing acceptance and mindfulness is a powerful strategy.

One of the greatest barriers to mindfulness is the expectation that the mind should become completely quiet. This misconception often leads people to believe they are "bad at meditation" when thoughts continue to appear. In reality, success is not measured by having no thoughts — it is measured by repeatedly bringing attention back to the present whenever the mind wanders.

Bringing It All Together

No single coping skill works for every person or every situation. Just as different tools are needed for different jobs, different coping skills may be more useful depending on the source and intensity of anxiety. Some strategies primarily calm the body, while others change unhelpful thinking patterns or redirect attention toward productive action.

The most effective approach is often a combination of several skills practiced consistently over time. Coping skills are not quick fixes; they are habits that strengthen emotional resilience through repetition. The more frequently they are practiced during calm moments, the more accessible they become during stressful ones.

Ultimately, coping skills do not promise a life without anxiety. Instead, they help individuals develop the confidence that, even when anxiety shows up, they possess the tools necessary to manage it effectively and continue moving toward the things that matter most.

5 Treatment Options

Anxiety is one of the most common and treatable mental health conditions. While everyone experiences anxiety from time to time, persistent anxiety that interferes with daily life often can be managed with professional treatment. Fortunately, there are several evidence-based approaches that have been shown to reduce anxiety symptoms and improve overall quality of life. The most effective treatment varies from person to person and often involves a combination of approaches.

Psychotherapy

Therapy is considered one of the most effective long-term treatments for anxiety because it helps individuals understand the factors contributing to their anxiety while developing practical skills to manage it.

One of the most well-researched approaches is Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), which focuses on identifying and changing unhelpful thinking patterns and behaviors that maintain anxiety. CBT also teaches coping skills such as cognitive restructuring, behavioral experiments, and gradual exposure to feared situations.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) takes a different approach by helping individuals accept uncomfortable thoughts and emotions rather than trying to eliminate them. ACT emphasizes mindfulness, psychological flexibility, and taking actions that align with personal values even when anxiety is present.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), while originally developed for emotion dysregulation, includes skills that can be highly effective for anxiety. DBT teaches mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness, helping individuals respond to anxiety in healthier ways.

Other therapeutic approaches, including mindfulness-based therapies, exposure therapy, and psychodynamic therapy, may also be appropriate depending on the individual's symptoms, history, and treatment goals.

Medication

Medication can be an effective option, particularly for individuals with moderate to severe anxiety or when anxiety significantly interferes with daily functioning. Medication does not "cure" anxiety but can reduce symptom intensity enough for individuals to engage more effectively in therapy and everyday life.

The most commonly prescribed medications are Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) and Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs). These medications are typically taken daily and often require several weeks before their full benefits are noticed.

Some individuals may also be prescribed benzodiazepines for short-term relief of acute anxiety. While these medications can be effective in the short term, they carry risks such as tolerance, dependence, sedation, and withdrawal symptoms, making them less appropriate for long-term management in most cases.

Medication decisions should always be made in consultation with a qualified medical provider who can evaluate potential benefits, side effects, medical history, and individual treatment needs.

Lifestyle Changes

Healthy lifestyle habits can significantly reduce anxiety and often enhance the effectiveness of therapy and medication. Regular physical exercise has consistently been shown to reduce anxiety symptoms by improving mood, regulating stress hormones, and promoting better sleep.

Maintaining consistent sleep habits, eating a balanced diet, limiting excessive caffeine and alcohol intake, and practicing stress-management techniques such as mindfulness, meditation, diaphragmatic breathing, and progressive muscle relaxation can all contribute to lower overall anxiety levels.

Although lifestyle changes alone may not fully resolve clinical anxiety, they provide an important foundation for emotional well-being and resilience.

Social Support

Strong social connections can serve as a protective factor against anxiety. Spending time with supportive family members, friends, or peers can reduce feelings of isolation, provide encouragement, and offer practical assistance during stressful periods.

For some individuals, support groups can also be valuable by providing a sense of community and the opportunity to learn from others facing similar challenges.

Finding the Right Combination

There is no single treatment that works best for everyone. Some individuals experience significant improvement through therapy alone, while others benefit from medication, lifestyle changes, or a combination of approaches. The most effective treatment plan is one that is individualized, evidence-based, and tailored to each person's unique symptoms, goals, and preferences.

With appropriate treatment and consistent effort, the vast majority of individuals with anxiety can learn to manage their symptoms effectively and lead healthy, productive, and fulfilling lives.
