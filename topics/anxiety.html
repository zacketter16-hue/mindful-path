2 What Is Anxiety?

Anxiety is an evolutionary survival mechanism our brain developed to keep us safe in the face of danger. It's not a sign of weakness or something that is inherently "wrong" with you. While this response was originally developed to help our ancestors survive physical threats, today it is often triggered by psychological challenges such as work, relationships, finances, health concerns, athletics, or social situations.

At its core, anxiety tends to emerge when three beliefs come together:

  1. We believe the outcome is important. The more something matters to us, the greater the emotional investment we have in the result. This could be a job interview, a championship game, a difficult conversation, an exam, or even how someone responds to a text message. When we attach significant meaning to an outcome, our brain naturally pays closer attention because it perceives that something valuable is at stake. In short, the greater the perceived threat, the more likely we are to experience anxiety.
  2. We believe the outcome is unpredictable. The human brain prefers certainty. We strive for safety, comfort, and familiarity. When we cannot accurately predict what will happen, our mind begins generating possibilities — many of which are negative. This uncertainty keeps the brain searching for answers and preparing for multiple scenarios. The less predictable we perceive a situation to be, the more vigilant and anxious we become.
  3. We believe the outcome is outside of our control. Perhaps the most powerful contributor to anxiety is the belief that we cannot influence what happens next. While we may be able to control our preparation, effort, attitude, or response, we cannot control other people's decisions, unexpected events, or final outcomes. When our attention becomes fixated on factors outside our control, the brain interprets the situation as a threat that we are unable to manage, causing anxiety to increase.

These three beliefs work together. If something is important but predictable and within your control, anxiety is usually manageable. If something is unpredictable but unimportant (think about a test worth 2% of your grade), it rarely creates significant distress. However, when an outcome feels highly important, highly uncertain, and outside your control, anxiety often reaches its highest levels.

Fortunately, this model also provides a roadmap for reducing anxiety. We can ask ourselves three questions:

  • Am I placing more importance on this outcome than it deserves?
  • Can I reduce uncertainty by preparing, gathering information, or making a plan?
  • What aspects of this situation are actually within my control, and how can I focus my energy there?

By shifting our attention toward controllable actions instead of uncontrollable outcomes, anxiety often begins to lose its intensity. Perspective reduces excessive importance. Preparation increases predictability. Focusing on our actions and responses restores a sense of control.

The goal is not to eliminate anxiety entirely. Some anxiety is both normal and useful — it can sharpen focus, increase motivation, and prepare the body for action. The goal is to understand why anxiety appears and to respond in a way that keeps it from controlling our thoughts, emotions, and behavior. When we recognize that anxiety is largely driven by our perceptions of importance, unpredictability, and control, we can begin changing those perceptions and, in turn, change our emotional experience.
