2 Understand Human Relationships

Human beings are inherently social creatures. From the moment we are born, we depend on relationships for survival, growth, and emotional well-being. Throughout life, our relationships continue to shape how we see ourselves, how we cope with stress, and how satisfied we feel with our lives. Research consistently demonstrates that healthy relationships are among the strongest predictors of happiness, physical health, longevity, and overall life satisfaction.

Logically, given how much we rely on and find happiness in our relationships, they are also the primary cause of pain and suffering in our lives. Navigating breakups, people coming in and out of our lives, and loss is incredibly challenging.

While every relationship is unique, healthy relationships tend to share many of the same characteristics. They are built not simply on love or attraction, but on trust, respect, communication, emotional safety, shared values, and mutual effort. Although no relationship is perfect, understanding what contributes to healthy relationships can help individuals build stronger connections while recognizing unhealthy patterns before they become more serious.

What Makes a Healthy Relationship?

Many people believe that compatibility alone determines whether a relationship will succeed. While compatibility certainly matters, long-term relationship success depends far more on how two people communicate, solve problems, and support one another during life's inevitable challenges. In other words, a lot of people can have fun and get along during the good times, but the meaningful relationships in our life that withstand the test of time are ones that get through the bad times with relative ease and even come out stronger on the other side.

Healthy relationships generally include several important characteristics:

  • Mutual trust
  • Open and honest communication
  • Respect for one another
  • Psychological and physical safety
  • Shared responsibility and roles
  • Healthy boundaries
  • Independence alongside togetherness
  • The ability to resolve conflict constructively

Healthy relationships allow both individuals to maintain their own identity while also building a meaningful life together. Each partner should feel comfortable expressing thoughts, emotions, opinions, and needs without fear of ridicule, manipulation, or retaliation.

An important misconception to address: healthy relationships are not ones without conflict. Disagreements are a normal part of every close relationship. The difference lies in how conflict is handled. Healthy couples and friends focus on solving problems together rather than trying to "win" arguments against one another. The respect and open-mindedness shown during difficult conversations can lead to a stronger relationship on the other end of it.

Love vs. Infatuation

Many relationships begin with intense attraction and excitement. This early stage is often referred to as infatuation or the "honeymoon phase." During this period, individuals may experience intense emotional highs, frequent thoughts about their partner, increased passion, and a tendency to overlook flaws or incompatibilities. In intimate relationships, this period may be highlighted by a high frequency of sex.

While this stage can be exciting, it is usually temporary. As relationships mature, intense excitement often gives way to a deeper form of connection built on trust, commitment, emotional intimacy, and mutual respect.

True love is less about constant excitement and more about consistently choosing to care for, support, and invest in another person over time. Healthy love allows two people to grow together while accepting one another as imperfect human beings.

Many people mistakenly interpret the fading of the honeymoon phase as evidence that the relationship is failing. In reality, for many couples, this transition marks the beginning of a more stable and meaningful connection. Ironically, moving on from the honeymoon phase and into a deeper, yet mundane part of the relationship is the best-case scenario for long-term relationships.

Communication

Communication is often considered the foundation of every healthy relationship. In addition to being able to openly say what's on your mind, it also active listening, validating emotions, expressing needs clearly, and approaching disagreements respectfully.

Healthy communication looks like:

  • Listening to understand rather than simply to respond
  • Expressing concerns calmly and respectfully
  • Taking responsibility for mistakes
  • Holding each other accountable clearly and directly if an expectation in the relationship is not adhered to
  • Asking questions rather than making assumptions
  • Validating another person's emotional experience even when disagreeing
  • Working together to solve problems – remembering that you're on the same team!

Poor communication, on the other hand, often involves criticism, defensiveness, contempt, avoidance, stonewalling, or passive-aggressive behavior. Over time, these patterns erode trust and emotional safety if they are not addressed.

One of the most important communication skills is recognizing that two people can experience the same situation differently. We perceive experiences based on a huge amount of highly subjective factors such as how we were raised, past outcomes, personality differences, and more. Understanding someone's perspective does not necessarily mean agreeing with it, but it demonstrates respect and strengthens emotional connection.

Healthy Boundaries

Boundaries define what behaviors are acceptable and expected within a relationship while protecting each person's emotional, physical, and psychological well-being. Healthy boundaries allow individuals to maintain their identity while remaining emotionally connected.

Boundaries may involve:

  • Respecting personal space
  • Maintaining friendships outside the relationship
  • Setting limits around behaviors that are previously agreed upon to be disrespectful
  • Protecting personal values
  • Balancing time together with time apart
  • The ability to have some sense of a personal/private life outside of the relationship

Many people mistakenly believe that setting boundaries is selfish or controlling. In reality, healthy boundaries reduce resentment, clarify expectations, and strengthen trust by creating emotional safety for both individuals.

Green Flags and Red Flags

Healthy relationships tend to display numerous "green flags" and "red flags" that indicate the likelihood of long-term compatibility.

Common green flags include:

  • Honest, direct communication
  • Consistent behavior
  • Accountability
  • Mutual respect
  • Emotional support
  • Reliability
  • Productive conflict resolution
  • Respect for boundaries
  • Shared values
  • Genuine happiness for one another's success

Common red flags include:

  • Controlling/manipulative behavior
  • Extreme jealousy or possessiveness
  • Frequent dishonesty
  • Pushed isolation from friends and family
  • Emotional, verbal, or physical abuse
  • Lack of accountability
  • Repeated violations of boundaries
  • Constant criticism or humiliation

While everyone makes mistakes, persistent patterns of disrespect, manipulation, or abuse should never be ignored. Note that many of the "red flag" behaviors derive from unhealthy attachment styles, which is addressed in the next module of this playbook.

Common Misconceptions About Relationships

Many expectations about relationships come from movies, social media, and unrealistic cultural constructs. This leads to unfair expectations of partners who will struggle to live up to the portrayal of "romance" and "happiness" shown in media.

One common misconception is that healthy couples never argue. In reality, conflict is unavoidable whenever two people share their lives. Successful relationships are defined less by the absence of conflict and more by how conflict is managed.

Another misconception (in part created by songs, shows, movies, etc.) is that terms such as "you complete me," or "I need you," or "you're my whole world," are positive. Healthy relationships involve two whole individuals choosing to share their lives, not relying on one another to provide identity, self-worth, or happiness. In the playbook on Addiction, I discuss how it's impossible to have a healthy relationship with anything (or anyone) that you NEED.

Similarly, the belief that jealousy, constant texting, or wanting to spend every moment together are signs of love. While these behaviors may initially feel flattering, they can sometimes reflect insecurity or unhealthy attachment rather than genuine intimacy (see the next module about attachment styles).

Finally, many believe that true love should always feel effortless. While healthy relationships should not feel chronically exhausting, every long-term relationship requires compromise, forgiveness, and intentional effort.

Building Strong Relationships

Healthy relationships are not built through grand romantic gestures but through countless small interactions repeated consistently over time. Showing appreciation, listening attentively, apologizing when appropriate, keeping promises, expressing affection, and supporting one another during difficult moments all contribute to lasting trust.

A healthy, successful relationship isn't one thing. It's a million little things.

Perhaps the most important lesson is that healthy relationships begin with healthy individuals. Developing self-awareness, emotional regulation, confidence, communication skills, and healthy boundaries not only improves personal well-being but also creates the foundation for stronger and more fulfilling relationships.

No relationship is perfect, and every couple experiences challenges. However, when two people are committed to honesty, respect, growth, and working together through difficulties, relationships can become one of the greatest sources of support, meaning, and fulfillment throughout life.

3 Attachment Styles

One of the most influential theories in relationship psychology is Attachment Theory. The gist is the idea that bonds we develop with our primary caregivers during childhood influence how we experience closeness, trust, vulnerability, and intimacy throughout adulthood.

Although attachment patterns begin early in life, they are not permanent personality traits. They're learned ways of relating to others that can improve with increased self-awareness and therapy. Understanding your attachment style is not about placing yourself into a fixed category or assigning blame for relationship difficulties. Rather, it allows you to recognize patterns that may either be strengthening or undermining your relationships. Education and awareness allows for intervention to be implemented to improve how you experience love and intimacy with the people you care about most.

Researchers generally describe four primary attachment styles: Secure, Anxious, Avoidant, and Fearful-Avoidant (Disorganized). Everyone possesses characteristics of each style to some degree, but most people tend to identify most strongly with one.

Secure Attachment

Individuals with a secure attachment style generally feel comfortable with both intimacy and independence. They are able to trust others without becoming overly dependent on them and are typically comfortable expressing their emotions, asking for support, and resolving conflict in healthy ways.

Securely attached individuals generally believe that they are worthy of love and that other people are trustworthy. Because of this, they are less likely to interpret minor relationship difficulties as signs that the relationship is ending. Since their perspective is typically healthy and based in reality, their actions tend to be positive for the relationship.

Strengths

People with secure attachment often:

  • Communicate openly and honestly
  • Express emotions appropriately
  • Trust their partner without excessive jealousy
  • Respect boundaries
  • Enjoy time alone
  • Recover from disagreements relatively quickly
  • Feel comfortable giving and receiving affection
  • Can tolerate temporary distance without assuming rejection

These qualities often create emotional safety, making secure individuals easier to build long-term relationships with.

Relationship Outcomes

Secure attachment is associated with the highest levels of relationship satisfaction, trust, commitment, and emotional stability. Couples with both partners having secure attachment generally experience fewer destructive conflicts and recover from disagreements more effectively than couples with insecure attachment patterns.

Keep in mind the misconception that conflict is bad and disagreements mean something is wrong with the relationship. Securely attached couples aren't immune to all relationship issues – they're just more likely to deal with them quicker and more efficiently.

Anxious Attachment

Individuals with an anxious attachment style often crave emotional connection but have a persistent fear of losing the relationship. They frequently worry about being abandoned, rejected, or not being "good enough" for their partner.

Because relationships are often central to their sense of emotional security, anxious individuals may be highly sensitive to changes in communication, affection, or perceived emotional distance.

For example, a partner taking longer than usual to respond to a text message may trigger thoughts such as:

  • "They're losing interest."
  • "I must have done something wrong."
  • "They're going to leave me."

Although these fears feel very real, they are often driven more by attachment anxiety than by objective evidence.

Strengths

Individuals with anxious attachment often possess many positive qualities within relationships. They are frequently highly affectionate, deeply caring, loyal, and motivated to resolve conflict quickly.

Many who have an anxious attachment style become exceptional partners when they develop greater emotional security and self-confidence through therapy and working on themselves.

Potential Challenges

Because of their fear of abandonment, anxious individuals may unintentionally engage in behaviors that create strain within relationships.

Examples include:

  • Frequently seeking reassurance
  • Overthinking interactions
  • Becoming overly dependent on their partner
  • Difficulty tolerating uncertainty
  • Feeling responsible for their partner's emotions
  • Becoming emotionally overwhelmed during conflict
  • Interpreting neutral situations as rejection

Ironically, these behaviors can sometimes overwhelm partners, increasing emotional distance and reinforcing the anxious individual's fear of abandonment. Therefore, many people with anxious attachment have experienced loss of relationships in the past, which in turn confirm their worries and lead to repeat behaviors in future relationships.

Relationship Outcomes

Anxious attachment can contribute to cycles of insecurity and emotional exhaustion if left unaddressed.

Breakups may be especially painful because those with anxious attachment often experience not only the loss of the relationship but also a profound loss of emotional security. They may spend months replaying conversations, questioning what went wrong, or struggling to let go.

Fortunately, increasing self-esteem, developing emotional regulation skills, and learning to tolerate uncertainty can significantly reduce anxious attachment over time.

Avoidant Attachment

Avoidant attachment individuals struggle with one of the most fundamental and common modern-day human experiences: The fear of vulnerability.

They generally value independence and self-reliance to such an extent that intimacy may begin to feel uncomfortable. Rather than fearing abandonment, they are often more concerned about losing their independence or becoming emotionally vulnerable.

Many avoidantly attached individuals genuinely care about their partners but have learned through past experiences that relying on others is unsafe, and therefore a deep discomfort grows the closer the relationship becomes.

When relationships become emotionally intense, they may instinctively withdraw rather than move closer.

Strengths

Avoidantly attached individuals often demonstrate several strengths, such as:

  • Ability to be independent
  • Comfortable making decisions
  • Less emotionally reactive during conflict

These qualities can be valuable in many areas of life.

Potential Challenges

Because vulnerability feels uncomfortable, avoidant individuals may struggle to fully engage emotionally within relationships, and could exhibit some challenging traits:

  • Difficulty expressing emotions
  • Needing significant more personal space, sometimes not "finishing an argument"
  • Suppressing emotional needs
  • Appearing emotionally distant

Partners sometimes interpret this behavior as a lack of love or commitment, even when the avoidant individual deeply cares about the relationship.

Relationship Outcomes

Avoidant attachment can create emotional distance that leaves partners feeling lonely or disconnected. Breakups often occur because partners eventually feel emotionally neglected or believe they are constantly being kept at arm's length.

Therapy could help avoidantly attached clients to have a greater comfort with vulnerability and emotional connection.

Fearful-Avoidant (Disorganized) Attachment

Fearful-avoidant attachment combines characteristics of both anxious and avoidant attachment. Individuals with this style often deeply desire intimacy while simultaneously fearing it.

Part of them wants closeness, another part is scared of getting hurt. As a result, their partners may feel confused, and the relationship may be unpredictable, and emotionally intense.

This attachment style is often a result of a child growing up with inconsistent care from their parents, traumatic experiences in childhood, and relationships throughout life in which the person they depended on hurt them deeply.

Strengths

Despite the challenges associated with this attachment style, individuals often possess many strengths. They are frequently:

  • Highly empathetic
  • Emotionally intuitive
  • Passionate
  • Deeply caring
  • Insightful
  • Resilient

Potential Challenges

Fearful-avoidant attachment often produces internal conflict. Individuals may:

  • Crave intimacy while pushing others away
  • Alternate between comfort with intimacy and need for isolation
  • Experience intense emotional reactions
  • Struggle to trust others
  • Fear abandonment while simultaneously fearing dependence
  • Become overwhelmed during conflict
  • Experience difficulty regulating emotions

Relationship Outcomes

Relationships involving fearful-avoidant attachment may become emotionally volatile and at times dangerous when partners are unable to understand and address the underlying attachment dynamics.

The internal conflict often leads to repeated cycles of reconnecting and distancing. Breakups can be especially difficult as those with fearful attachment may have deep emotional wounds triggered by the ending of the relationship while simultaneously craving reconciliation.

Can Attachment Styles Change?

One of the biggest misconceptions about attachment theory is that attachment styles are fixed for life. Research suggests this is not true. Attachment patterns are learned, and learned patterns and beliefs can change.

People often become more secure through:

  • Individual therapy
  • Healing from past trauma
  • Developing emotional regulation skills
  • Building self-confidence
  • Learning healthy communication
  • Practicing vulnerability in safe relationships

The goal is not to become a "perfect" partner but to become increasingly secure in how we relate to ourselves and others.

Understanding Attachment in Relationships

Attachment styles do not determine whether a relationship will succeed or fail. They influence how individuals respond to closeness, conflict, vulnerability, and emotional stress.

For example:

  • Two securely attached individuals often communicate effectively and recover from conflict relatively quickly.
  • An anxious partner paired with an avoidant partner may create a "pursuer-distancer" cycle in which one partner seeks greater closeness while the other withdraws, unintentionally reinforcing one another's fears.
  • Two anxious partners may create a relationship with high emotional intensity, frequent reassurance seeking, and fear of abandonment.
  • Two avoidant partners may maintain harmony for a period of time but struggle to develop deeper emotional intimacy.

Final Thoughts

Attachment styles help explain many of the recurring patterns people experience in relationships, but they do not define who we are or determine our future. Healthy relationships are built through self-awareness, communication, emotional regulation, trust, and a willingness to grow.

Perhaps the most encouraging finding from attachment research is that emotional security is something that can be developed. Regardless of where someone begins, increasing self-awareness and practicing healthier relationship skills can lead to stronger, more satisfying, and more secure relationships throughout life.

4 Understand Breakups

Introduction

Nearly everyone will experience a significant breakup at some point in their life. While relationships can bring joy, connection, and security, their ending can trigger one of the most emotionally painful experiences a person can face. You could struggle to eat, sleep, concentrate at work or school, and struggle to complete simple tasks. Experiences can also include intense anxiety, sadness, anger, guilt, or even physical pain.

If you've ever thought:

  • "Why can't I stop thinking about them?"
  • "Why does this hurt so much?"
  • "Will I ever feel normal again?"
  • "Why do I still miss someone who wasn't good for me?"

You're not alone.

A breakup is far more than simply losing a partner. It means losing a best friend, daily routines, future plans, emotional security, shared memories, and sometimes even a part of your identity. Understanding why breakups are so painful is often the first step toward healing.

Why Breakups Hurt So Much

Research using brain imaging has shown that rejection and heartbreak activate areas involved in processing distress, including regions that are also active when people experience physical injuries.

This explains why heartbreak doesn't simply feel "sad." People often describe a tightness in their chest, a pit in their stomach, loss of appetite, fatigue, and nausea. Your brain interprets the loss of an important attachment figure as a significant threat, and signals distress to the body.

From an evolutionary perspective, this makes sense. Humans evolved as social creatures. Being separated from important relationships historically reduced chances of survival. As a result, our brains developed powerful systems designed to keep us close to people we depend on.

Heartbreak is not evidence that you're weak. It is evidence that your brain formed an attachment, and needs to process and adjust to the loss. In other words, your brain hasn’t determined that you’ll be okay yet, and is trying to keep you on guard until it does.

The Loss Is Bigger Than the Person

Models of managing grief and loss may apply when processing a breakup. But one of the biggest misconceptions about breakups is that you’re simply grieving the person you lost. In addition to that, the grief spans across multiple losses occurring simultaneously, such as:

  • Daily routines and stability
  • Physical intimacy and affection
  • Emotional support
  • Mutual friends
  • Family connections
  • Vision of the future

On a complicated but significant note, the breakup may represent the death of the future you imagined. You are forced to let go of the possibilities that never happened. Dealing with this kind of loss feels hollow, lonely, and hard to describe.

Why Your Brain Keeps Thinking About Your Ex

It can be frustrating to not be able to stop thinking about your former partner, even if you were the one that ultimately ended the relationship. This is completely normal, as your brain is designed to focus on and attempt to solve unfinished problems.

When a relationship ends, especially if it’s unexpectedly, it creates countless unanswered questions:

  • How could the outcome have been different?
  • Did I do something wrong?
  • Is there still an opportunity to fix it?
  • Will they come back?
  • Will I ever be okay again?

The brain continues searching for answers because uncertainty is psychologically uncomfortable. Unfortunately, rumination rarely produces clarity, and often creates more questions than it solves. It often keeps emotional wounds open and distracts from engaging in thoughts and actions that would help us move forward.

The goal of recovery is not having the answer every question, it’s about learning to tolerate the disappointment and uncertainty.

The Chemistry of Heartbreak

Romantic love involves several powerful brain chemicals including:

Dopamine

Associated with, pleasure, the reward system, and motivation. Being with someone you love often becomes one of your brain's strongest sources of dopamine. After a breakup, this reward system suddenly disappears, leaving a void in the typical source of happiness and a noticeable shortage of supply at first.

Many people experience symptoms similar to withdrawal, which is why after a breakup someone may be more likely to seek dopamine in other ways such as substances, sex, or other vices.

Oxytocin

Often called the "bonding hormone." Physical affection, trust, and intimacy strengthen emotional attachment. When that bond is suddenly removed, people often feel lonely even when surrounded by others, and could feel a sense of hopelessness and isolation that may persist while the brain adjusts to a new normal.

Stress Hormones

Following a breakup, cortisol often increases. Elevated stress hormones contribute to anxiety, difficulty sleeping, trouble concentrating, irritability, and fatigue. This helps explain why people often feel mentally and physically exhausted after heartbreak. As discussed in the Anxiety playbook, managing high levels of cortisol by exercising or talk therapy may help reduce the impact.

Grief Is Not Linear

People often expect recovery to move in a straight line. To feel a little bit better everyday until you’re over it.

Instead, healing tends to look more like waves. One week you may feel hopeful, but then a song, picture, or smell brings a memory back that is attached with intense sadness. It may feel like a setback, but it’s a completely natural part of moving on.

Check out the Grief and Loss Playbook for more information, but the short of it is when I have a client struggling with grief, we focus on three indications of whether we’re making progress. We look at whether the intense negative emotions are reducing in:

  • Frequency – how often they’re occurring
  • Severity – how significant and debilitating the experience it is when they occur
  • Duration – how long they’re lasting when they occur

Like a stock, how you’re doing sometimes requires zooming out. You could have a bad day or even a bad week, but when we zoom out, are you generally trending in the right direction? If they answer is yes, then allow some space and time for bad days – it’s natural. If the answer is no, then certainly reach out for help and support as you navigate this difficult time.

Identity After a Breakup

Long-term relationships become integrated into our identity. People begin thinking in terms of "we" instead of "me." After a breakup, many people wonder, "Who am I without this relationship?"

Recovery often involves rebuilding an independent identity.

This might include:

  • Rediscovering hobbies
  • Reconnecting with friends
  • Pursuing personal goals
  • Developing new routines
  • Learning to enjoy solitude

One of the healthiest outcomes after heartbreak is becoming someone you genuinely enjoy spending time with. Developing an independent identity and being a person you’re proud of and confident in reduces the impact that breakups, rejection, embarrassment, etc. have on us.

Whether you’re currently single, in a relationship, or anywhere in between, it’s always the right time to invest in yourself, identify your strengths and interests, and live a fulfilling life on your own terms. A romantic relationship should compliment that, but it should never replace your individuality.

The Trap of Idealizing an Ex

After a breakup, the brain often remembers the highlights while minimizing the difficulties. This is called selective memory. "They were perfect." "I'll never find someone like them." Or at a minimum we may downplay the issues in the relationship that led to the breakup in the first place, leaving us to wonder what happened, why, and whether we made a mistake.

A helpful exercise is writing three lists:

  • What did I enjoy about the relationship?
  • What did the relationship need that wasn’t there?
  • What did the relationship involve that it shouldn’t have?

This gives reasonable credit to what was positive about the relationship while acknowledging the issues.

Closure: A Common Misunderstanding

You may believe that you won’t be able to heal until you receive closure from their former partner. Realistically, you may never get that answer you’re looking for, that perfect explanation, that acknowledgement of all you’ve done for them, or final well wishes.

The good news: you don’t need it. Closure, like forgiveness, comes from within.

That explanation, comment, or final goodbye is highly unlikely to have the positive impact you think it will. Sometimes the other person can’t explain their emotions to you, as they struggle to comprehend them themselves. Sometimes it’d simply open wounds that’ve been slowly healing. Sometimes it just wouldn’t change anything. Loss is loss, and regardless of the why of it, most of the time the answers we’re seeking won’t satisfy us to know.

Waiting indefinitely for perfect closure can delay recovery. Once again, healing isn’t about finding the answers – it’s about learning to live without them.

Warning Signs That Additional Support May Be Needed

Heartbreak is painful, but sometimes it develops into a more significant mental health concern.

Consider seeking support from a mental health professional if symptoms include:

  • Persistent inability to function at work or school that lasts across several months
  • Severe depression (check Depression Playbook for more info) lasting several weeks
  • Significant weight loss or weight gain
  • Persistent insomnia
  • Panic attacks
  • Feelings of hopelessness that culminate to thoughts of self-harm or suicide

Needing support is not a sign of failure. You’re not “losing the breakup.” You’re investing in the new version of you that deserves to be happy as you move forward.

Key Takeaways

  • A breakup is not simply the end of a relationship, it’s the loss of routines, identity, dreams, security, and connection.
  • The intensity of heartbreak reflects the strength of human attachment, not personal weakness.
  • Recovery is rarely linear. Emotional waves are expected, and setbacks do not mean you are failing or “losing the breakup.”
  • Healing involves accepting uncertainty, rebuilding your identity, reconnecting with supportive people, and gradually creating a life that feels meaningful on its own.
  • Although heartbreak can feel permanent in the moment, the vast majority of people recover. It may change you, as all of our experiences do, but many eventually look back on painful breakups as turning points that helped them develop greater resilience, healthier relationship patterns, and a clearer understanding of what they truly need from future partners.
